# 2. NOVEL PAGE  (/novel)

## 2.1 Novel hero — the rich scene

> Visual: the cinematic rainy image — a small Japanese commuter station at dusk, warm café light
> against rain-grey, a hint of the platform, windows doubling reflections. `[PENDING: final novel
> hero image — regenerate in the Japanese register; see image prompt note in the Next Session Pack.]`

**Title:** *They Only Came When It Rained*
**Launch:** Coming August 2026

**Epigraph (a real line from the book, spoiler-free):**
> *"The glass walls reflect people twice, so everyone looks like they're going somewhere and coming
> back at the same time."*

## 2.2 What it's about

> Beside the ticket gates of a small commuter station, a young man works the late shift. He has the
> same single espresso at the same hour while unconsciously folding paper cranes between customers.
> Then, on certain rainy nights, a narrow passage opens where no passage should be, onto a platform
> only the lost can find: a woman who no longer drinks her coffee, a man who has stopped going home,
> a boy waiting for a train that never comes. He sits with each of them, listens, and writes it all
> down in a small brown notebook — until the questions he asks his visitors begin, very quietly, to
> sound like questions meant for him.

## 2.3 The feeling  (describe, don't label)

> A quiet, contemplative novel about people who can't move on — and the near-invisible difference
> between waiting for your life and living it. Warm at its center, with a current of something colder
> and stranger running underneath. If you lose yourself in *Before the Coffee Gets Cold* or *What You
> Are Looking For Is in the Library*, this was written for the same hour of the night.

*Annotation:* genre is conveyed by **feeling + comps**, not a badge. The comps do the shelving so the
copy never has to say "cozy."

## 2.4 Atmosphere strip  (surface motifs only — safe)

> Short, evocative fragments (set as captions / a quiet row, not explained):
> - Rain that never falls the same way twice.
> - A single espresso, in at six, finished by half past.
> - Paper cranes folded from the paper people leave behind.
> - A girl in a yellow raincoat, in every Tuesday for her chamomile.
> - A brown notebook, and entries that trail off before they end.

*Annotation:* these **evoke** the world without decoding any of it. The girl, the cranes, and the
notebook appear only as texture — their meanings stay off the page.

# 4. ABOUT PAGE  (/about)

> Visual: author photo, primary placement. `[PENDING: window-lit, muted-tone author photo]`

## 4.1 Bio  (opens with a FACT — never the slogan)

> Meher Yar Khan is a Pakistani-Finnish writer and digital nomad. His first novel, *They Only Came
> When It Rained* — a quiet, contemplative story about people who can't move on — arrives in August
> 2026. By training he's an electrical and software engineer, and he builds writing tools, including
> Compare My Drafts, a web app for taking a draft from a messy first pass to a finished one.

## 4.2 A note from me  (first person — the personal, concrete part)

> I owe the reading habit to my grandfather. It started with *Goosebumps*, ran straight into fantasy
> and science fiction, and never really stopped — Pratchett, Gaiman, Tolkien, and the self-published
> worlds of Steven Campbell's *Hard Luck Hank*, which I mostly listen to now, because audiobooks are
> how a busy life still gets to read.
>
> I wrote my first story when I was ten: a different monster on every page, one after another, until
> I ran out of paper. I've written several manuscripts since. None of them found a traditional
> publisher, so I'm putting this one into the world myself — which feels, finally, like the right way
> to do it.
>
> I write between countries, usually with a coffee going. It's probably no accident that this first
> novel ended up living in a café.

*Annotation:* the bio earns trust with **facts** (nationality, the dated debut, the engineering
background, the shipped tool); the note earns *warmth* with **one concrete image** — the
ten-year-old's monster pages — and the true coffee → café throughline. The self-publishing line is
owned confidently, not apologized for.
